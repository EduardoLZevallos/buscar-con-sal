"The pride of US politicians has been hurt by what Nicaragua has been able to do being a small country"

Enviado por tortilla el Lun, 07/04/2025 - 15:18

Interview by Ken Yale with Harold Urbina, March 15th 2025

Ken Yale: Okay, so here we are on the 15th of March, 2025. Can you tell us your name and your role, or a title that you would like to use for identification in this interview?

Harold Urbina: I think that for this interview, I will identify myself as Harold Urbina. I am part of the team of Jubilee House Community and Casa Benjamin Linder, which is the project for art, education, and solidarity of Jubilee House Community.

Ken Yale: Okay. And tell us where are we right now when we're recording this interview.

Harold Urbina: Now, we are here at Laguna de Apoyo. We are, in fact, in one of the resorts in Laguna de Apoyo, which is in Catarina municipality. It's one of the top tourist attractions here in the southern part of Nicaragua.

Ken Yale: Okay, and tell us about your own family, your educational background, and some of the people who influenced you as a young person in Nicaragua.

Harold Urbina: Okay, first I'm going to talk to you about my parents. My father, Rafael Urbina Brizuela, was a radio announcer. He worked in a radio station for his whole life. He passed away last year. And my mother, Martha Cruz, has always been a housewife. She's been only working at home. When it comes to my political background, I have to say that my parents didn't have a direct influence with my political life, because they were just ordinary citizens. But they were never against my involvement in the Sandinista Front since I was a child. On the contrary, they were always supporting me to do whatever I was doing.

And I grew up in a popular neighborhood in Managua, in the district number two of Managua. It's a neighborhood close to Monseñor Lezcano neighborhood. And I was attending a public school for my primary education, and then I also went to a public school for my high school or secondary education. And when I was in primary school, when I was 10 years old, I decided to become a member of what was called at that time, the Sandinista Children Association, Asociación de Niños Sandinistas. The abbreviation was ANS, and the name of the association was Luis Alfonso Velasquez, who was a child, a boy who was killed by the National Guard of Somoza back in the 70's. So this boy, Luis Alfonso, he became a national hero, and also like the child symbol of the Sandinista Revolution. And I was proud to be a member of the ANS, of this association. And I was an active member when I was in fifth and sixth grade of primary education in 1983 and 1984.

And I also have to say that another big influence for becoming a member of the Sandinista Front since I was a child was the fact that I was able to witness the impact of the Nicaraguan society that the National Literacy Crusade had. And I could meet some of my neighbors and friends who were older, of course, because by the time that we had the triumph of the revolution, I was only six years old, and by the time that the National Literacy Crusade took place, I was only seven. But I could see my friends returning from the mountains saying, "Oh, it's a whole new world that we have just discovered. We just realized how the rural areas and the cities were separated by the dictatorship. So now we know how they produce for us. Now we know how the gallo pinto that we eat every day at home is produced. Now we know how every time we are drinking a cup of coffee, how hard it is for them to produce coffee in the mountains." So I was understanding things that I wasn't able to realize before, and that impacted me as well.

So I decided to join the political party. Not a party, because at that time, the Sandinista Front was in the process to make its transition from a guerilla movement into a political party. But anyway, I understood that the Sandinista leadership, the one that was leading the revolution in the National Literacy Crusade, was also connected to another huge, huge improvement in the life of the Nicaraguan people, which was the Agrarian Reform. I never saw a lot of small farmers being for the first time in their life, being allowed to have a piece of land. And you can imagine how it was a huge change in the life of people having a piece of land and being allowed to learn how to read and write. So obviously, for any nation in the world, it is a big change. So the very start of the revolution was something that was having an impact, an impact on people's life, because it was like coming from having nothing into another situation in which you were allowed to have the rights you never were allowed to have. And so I was really happy to support that process, and that's why I became a member of the Sandinista Children's Association.

But I also have to mention things at school. I started to learn about Nicaraguan history. I was also inspired by the ideas of Sandino and the ideas of Carlos Fonseca, the founder of the Sandinista Front. I didn't have that information available before, so I was like discovering a new world, and I was happy to be part of that.

Ken Yale: Let me go back for a minute just to place this in history. So roughly what year was the Literacy Campaign and roughly what year was the Agricultural Reform?

Harold Urbina: Okay, both the Agrarian Reform and the Literacy Campaign, they both took place in 1980. In the year before, in 1979 we had the triumph of the revolution. And I have to tell you this. I was only six years old, but I still remember today, as if it was yesterday, that I was sent by my mother to the corner in my neighborhood to buy tortillas. And when I was right there buying the tortillas for my house, I saw a man who came into the house wearing a red and black bandana covering his face, and he said, "We won, the march to victory will never end." Él dijo: "Ganamos, la marcha hacia la victoria no se detiene."

So I wasn't able to understand the political background of that moment, but I could see the happiness on the faces of the people, and I could see how each person inside that house came to this man and they were all giving him hugs and celebrating. So I knew it was something very big, important. So I went back to my place, and when I was walking back home, I stopped on the corner to see the parade of guerilla fighters. They were all coming from, I assume from Leon, because my house is located in the western part of Managua in district number two. So I saw the parade coming down the western part of Nicaragua, and I could see hundreds of youngsters on top of tanks and pick up trucks in different kinds of vehicles. And they were all screaming, celebrating, and people were standing in both sides of the street, just like welcoming them.

Then after stopping to see the parade, I went into my house, and I saw all of my family members. They were all together around a small 12 inch black and white television, and I was able to see the first speech of the revolution on that day. So I wasn't able to understand the content of the speech at that time, but I could see again, the happiness on the face of my family, so I knew it was something powerful. And I think it's that moment I became engaged with the revolution. Obviously, later I was able to learn a lot. But I think that specific moment marked my life in a way. And again, then the next year, in 1980 we have the Agrarian Reform and the Literacy Campaign, both processes were together. So I think that was a good strategy that we used because it impacted the life of people, both processes at the same time.

Ken Yale: So just to be clear, when you say the revolution won in 1979, just very briefly explain what that means. Who was it that won, and who was it that was defeated?

Harold Urbina: Okay, I have to mention that America is not the US. America is a continent, and it was first invaded by the Spanish Empire in 1492. And right after we had our independence in this region, in Central America and Mexico, we had an independence from Spain in 1821. But to be honest, it was a false independence. Because what happened was that the first Europeans who came to colonize, they went back home, but they kept people they trusted. In fact, their descendants, European descendants, became the new rulers of the Americas.

So in the case of Central America and Mexico, it was the same process. White people became the rulers of Central America and Mexico, and we didn't have a real reason to celebrate because indigenous people were still oppressed by the white people. But there was something even worse for the Central American people and the Latin American people in general, because our independence was in 1821, but right a couple of years later, in 1823, the US came up with the Monroe Doctrine. And they started to find ways to control and oppress the people here in this hemisphere. They were using the justification that they were chosen by God to take care and to protect this part of the world, and that the US wasn't going to allow any European superpower to put its hands in Latin America, and we were the new target of the US interest.

And even before the first US intervention as a government, we had a man from the US who came to Nicaragua, whose name was William Walker. He came in 1855. He was sponsored by a man in the US, a rich man in the US called Cornelius Vanderbilt. And he sponsored a trip of this man because they wanted to find a way to build a canal here in Nicaragua to unite the Pacific Ocean with the Caribbean Sea in order to compete, of course, with the Panama Canal.

But when William Walker came here, he identified one of the weaknesses of the politicians at that time. Because I forgot to mention that when we had this independence in 1821, what happened, in fact, was that the sense of democracy that was promoted at the time was that the white people, the European descendants, they had two political parties, the Liberal Party and the Conservative Party. But the liberal was not liberal. In fact, was just another conservative party. But they were just shifting terms in power. I often compare this situation with what people in the US are facing today, that I don't see any difference between Republicans and Democrats, but that's another story. But the point is that here we were not having real elections or real democracy.

So when Walker came, he realized that, that the politicians were just interested in their own benefit. So by identifying that weakness, he said, "Oh, I'm going to make myself the president of Nicaragua." And he did that. He became the president of Nicaragua. We were even having English as the official language of Nicaragua for almost one year. But the interest or the ambitions of William Walker was beyond that. Because he said, "Well, I know that in the in the US, in the northern states, slavery has been already abolished. But what I also know is that in the southern states, they still keep slavery. So I can take indigenous people from Nicaragua and from Costa Rica and Honduras and export them to the US as slaves." And that's what he was planning to do.

And that's why, for the first time, Honduras and Costa Rica were supporting the resistance movement here in Nicaragua, in order to defeat William Walker. And we did. And there's a famous battle that took place in San Jacinto, a farm close to Managua. And when we defeated the first filibusters that were brought here by Walker, we consider that date the first, not just the first defeat of the US, but also, we consider the 14th of September as our Independence Day. So we have two independence celebrations in Nicaragua. First the 14th of September, because on the 14th of September 1856 we defeated William Walker, and then we have the 15 of September, which is the date to remember the independence of Central America in 1821.

So that's basically what happened. So keeping in the timeline, I have to say that the first real US military intervention in Nicaragua took place in 1910. Because, again, the politicians in Nicaragua were fighting each other for their own interest, and they were shifting turns in power. So under that framework, it was a point when Nicaragua was having as a nation, many, many, many civil wars. Perhaps we can count more than 12 times in our history when we were having civil wars. But they were wars between liberals and conservatives. The same story for many years. So I think that, in fact, what happened before the US intervention in 1910 was that the Conservatives were calling for the support of US forces to come and intervene in our domestic or political issues.

And they came. The Marines came in 1910. Instead of facilitating a peace process here, they just took control of the whole Nicaraguan nation. And I remember that there were just a few people fighting against this intervention. And one of them was a general, a liberal General, whose name was Benjamin Zeledón. He was fighting against this intervention, but he was defeated. And when the US Marines killed General Benjamin Zeledón, the body of Benjamin Celegon was tied up to horses. It was shown on the streets for people to see what was going to happen to anyone daring to fight against the US Army. And one of the boys on the streets that saw Benjamin Zeledón's body was Sandino.

So Sandino was inspired by the sacrifice of Benjamin Zeledón, and he said, "I have to do something. How come I will accept passively that we have a foreign intervention in our country?" And Sandino was not a military person, he was not an academic, so he was just learning on his own how to do something against the US intervention. So Augusto Sandino, who is our national hero, he was traveling. He went to Central America, he went to Mexico. And he learned about the struggle of other nations, and he was inspired by the trade union movements from Spain and from other parts of Europe. And he learned that when they were organizing strikes and demonstrations, they were using one red flag and one black flag in order to represent their struggle. So Sandino said, "Oh, if I ever have an army, I will combine these two flags." And that's how we have now the red and black flag. And the red means the blood of the people that have been dying, giving their lives for the struggle. And the black represents the dead, the sadness of the people that have been killed in the struggle.

Anyway, the point is that Sandino was trying to find a way to resist. And he organized an army of small farmers and indigenous people to fight against the US. Intervention. And Sandino founded his army in 1927, 1927 is the year when Sandino founded his army. And his army was, in fact, founded on the second of September, 1927. And we currently in Nicaragua celebrate the foundation of the Nicaraguan army on the second of September every year because of Sandino's army.

And Sandino was defeated many times. But by the lessons he learned, because he was not a military person, he was able to develop something called guerrilla warfare strategy. And when he was implementing this strategy, he was able to easily defeat the US Army, because they were only used to conventional wars. They didn't know how to fight in the mountains. So it was easy for Sandino to prepare his actions in the mountains. And you know, the US Marines were not used to this kind of weather in the mountains, tropical weather. So Sandino started to defeat more and more the US forces, until the point that between 1927 to 1931 the US forces were defeated.

So in the next few years, in 1932 and 1933, the US Marines after they were defeated by Sandino, the US troops stated that they were going to withdraw from Nicaraguan territory because they were tired, they were not going anywhere. So they said, "we're going, we're going to go back home." Sandino was really committed to peace and really committed to the benefit of the Nicaraguan people. So he said, "if the US troops are going to withdraw from Nicaraguan territory, if we are going to be just dealing with the problems of the Nicaraguan people, I don't see any reason for me to keep arm in the mountains. So I'm open to have a peace process."

The president of Nicaragua at that time, who was Sacasa, Juan Bautista Sacasa, he said, "Sandino, okay, you can come to the capital, to Managua, and we can start some peace talks to find a way to solve this." And Sandino came and he made an agreement with Sacasa to give the weapons of his army, and they were unarmed. The army of Sandino was unarmed, and they were open to have peace. And Sandino was even invited many times to come to Managua between 1933 and 1934.

And last time he was invited here to Managua in 1934 in February, one year after the peace talks started, Sandino was having a dinner with the chief of the National Guard at the time, who was Anastasio Somoza García. But let me tell you that the National Guard is an armed force that was created by the US military occupation. In Nicaragua we didn't, have an army, and for the years that we were under the rule of liberal and conservative parties, they didn't have any Nicaraguan army. In fact, when the US Marines came and Nicaragua was occupied by them, they created the National Guard. And because the National Guard was only protecting the US interest, they gave an assignment, a task, to the chief of the National Guard, who was Anastasio Somoza García. And that task was to kill Sandino.

As I told you before, Sandino was really committed to peace. He was really smart, because he was talking about sovereignty, he was talking about self-determination, dignity of the Nicaraguan and the Indigenous people. He was even talking about some of the human rights that were not recognized at the time. Because we're talking about the 1930's, and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights is from 1948. So the point is that Sandino was really advanced, but at the same time he was really naive, because he came to Managua to have a dinner with Somoza, and he brought all of the top people of his army. And when they came to Managua, they were all killed the same night.

And there was only one of the generals of Sandino's army, who was the youngest general, who was able to escape that night. His name was Santos López. And years later, Santos López became the link between Sandino and the Sandinista Front. Because Santos López met Carlos Fonseca, who was later the founder of the Sandinista Front. And before I forget, when Somoza assassinated Sandino, Somoza became the president of Nicaragua because he was awarded this power position by the US government at that time, because he completed the task they gave him. And that's why there's a famous quote by President Roosevelt saying, "Somoza is a son of a bitch, but he's ours." Because as soon as Somoza became the new president, after being just the chief of the National Guard, he became a dictator and he was oppressing and repressing the Nicaraguan people. And he had the open and total support of the US government at that time, and he knew it. And that's why he felt empowered to do whatever he wanted.

Ken Yale: So basically, the Somoza dictatorship takes over after the formation of the National Guard and the assassination of Sandino. How long does the Somoza family rule Nicaragua?

Harold Urbina: Yeah, the Somoza family was ruling Nicaragua for almost 50 years, because they were in power from 1934 until 1979, when we had the triumph of the revolution. And when I'm talking about the triumph of the revolution, I mean that the Sandinista Front was a guerrilla movement that was founded in 1961 under the framework of the national liberation movements in the world, as in Asia, Africa, in some other parts of the world, where people were still living as colonies from Europe. The same here in Nicaragua. We were talking about the national liberation of Nicaragua. FSLN, in Spanish, Frente Sandinista de Liberación Nacional, in English is National Liberation Sandinista Front, is the name of the guerrilla movement that we founded, inspired by Sandino's ideas.

And as I was saying before, Santos Lopez, who was a general of Sandino's army, he met Carlos Fonseca, and he was able to transfer all of the political and ideological legacy of Sandino to Carlos Fonseca. And the Sandinista Front was not only inspired in Sandino's legacy, it was also Inspired by Marxism and all of the socialist ideas of the revolution in the Soviet Union. In fact, Carlos Fonseca went to Moscow in the 60's, at the end of the 50s, and he was able to witness what the Soviet revolution was. And he returned to Nicaragua inspired. Carlos Fonseca was also visiting Cuba many times. Many of the leaders of the Sandinista Revolution were trained in Cuba.

So there was a political influence by Marxism, as I was saying. But also the Sandinista Revolution was not only inspired by Marxism, it was also inspired by liberation theology. Many Christians became supporters of the Sandinista Front. And that's how, since the 60's, we were already working in the direction of thinking about educating people, and we were using the different tools of popular education to transfer knowledge to people. Because the principle of popular education is that as long as you have a community or a population that is educated, they can transform, they can understand, reflect on the life they have, and transform reality. So we understood since the very beginning that education is power, and that a population that is well informed is able to make better decisions. And this combination of Marxism with the positive values of Christianity, and all of the person-to-person support, based on the Christian values of solidarity, all of these combined, I think were key factors that contributed to the growing and development of the Sandinista Front in the 60's and 70's.

And I can also mention that in ‘78 there was an important action of the Sandinista Front, because the members of the Parliament at that time were all taken as hostages by a special group or cell of the Sandinista Front. And after this action, many of the leaders of the Sandinista Front who were in prison were released. And it was a good way for the Sandinista Front to recover from many different actions in the previous years when the Sandinista guerrillas were massacred by the National Guard.

So I think that between August ‘78 until July ‘79 was the period in which, after accumulating forces or power, the Sandinista Front returned to fight with more energy and with more strategies that were successful for defeating the National Guard.

So I have to mention that the assault of the National Parliament was a very key action for the Sandinista Front, not only for releasing the prisoners that were in in the prisons of the National Guard, but also, because the Sandinista Front was known worldwide for this action. I can recommend you read a book written by the Colombian writer, Gabriel García Márquez, about this action. The book is called The Assault, El Asalto is the name in Spanish. It was like a tribute that Gabriel Garcia Márquez made to the Nicaraguan Revolution. Because I think that book was written in ‘82 or ‘83.

So the point is that the Sandinistas were successful in many of the military actions they were doing back in the 70's. But before the 70's, I have to mention something really, really important. I forgot to mention when I was talking about my influences in the past. I told you about Sandino and Carlos Fonseca, but there's a national hero whose name was Julio Buitrago.

Julio Buitrago was a 20-year-old person. He was the best student of the School of Law at that time here in Managua. And he was a leader of the urban resistance of the Sandinista Front, and he was organizing the resistance in the neighborhoods of Managua. But he was detected by the National Guard when he was hiding in a safe house. So he was so committed with the struggle that he was asking the comrades that were with him operating in this specific group, that they should go, that they should escape.

And he stayed in the place. It was a house with a second floor. So what he did was that he was having a machine gun, and he was already aware that the National Guards were outside the house. But what he didn't know was that the National Guard thought it was a big group of Sandinistas inside the house. So then they called on full support, and there were many Jeeps or National Guard vehicles outside the house. They even brought a tank and they were even using a plane to bomb the house. And Somoza made a mistake, because he was inviting journalists from local and international media to come and give coverage live, to broadcast live what was an action of the National Guard in order to protect the Nicaraguan population from a terrorist group, because that's the way they were calling the Sandinista Front back in the 70's, and in the 60's in this case.

So then a lot of journalists came to cover the action. And when they were finally able to complete the action, the house was destroyed totally, they entered the house, and they only found Julio Buitrago, who was this 20-year-old boy. He was basically massacred alone. And outside there were more than 200 National Guards. So it was hundreds of guards. There was this tank and the airplane, all of this movement for attacking only one person. So the result for the Somoza dictatorship was the opposite. People knew the high level of repression because in the body of Julio, they found a lot of documentation, a lot of documents, of papers talking about Somoza and how he was oppressing and repressing the people.

So instead of being a benefit, it was something that affected the dictatorship. And for most kids in Nicaragua, Julio Buitrago became like a real hero, because it was in the news, and we were just thinking, "Oh, one boy against hundreds of National Guards, one person." So, yeah, it had a high influence on most people. And if you go to the museum of Julio Buitrago, which is located in the western part of Managua, the same district I live, district number two, you will see that on one of the walls, there's a poster with a quote from President Daniel Ortega saying, "I still remember when Managua kids were playing on the street and some of them were always having a role play. And one of the characters they were using for the role play was Julio Buitrago." They were saying, "Oh, I want to play Julio Buitrago," because they knew what happened.

So I think in my own case, he's one of my heroes. So we didn't care about Spider Man, Superman, Batman, or all of these cartoon characters when we already had Julio Buitrago, a real hero. And I think that inspired me. And politically, as I said, if we have a list of top actions representing the Sandinista Front, we can think of the 15 of July, 1969 when Julio Buitrago was killed, and then the 22nd of August, 1978 when the Parliament was taken by the Sandinista Front.

By describing this, now I have to come to another point that I think was key for getting the triumph. And it was also in ‘78 when a big journalist was assassinated. He was Pedro Joaquin Chamorro. He was the owner of La Prensa, the largest newspaper in Nicaragua at that time, and he was publicly talking about how Somoza was repressing people. And he was really critical of Somoza. I think perhaps not because he was supporting the Sandinista Front, but because the same oligarchy, the same rich people in Nicaragua, were feeling affected by Somoza at that time, to the point that they themselves were against Somoza. And Somoza just sent someone to kill him.

So I think it was a strong message for ordinary people, because they were saying, "You see, if Somoza is killing rich families in Nicaragua and he doesn't care, what is he going to do against you, who are just a teacher, a worker?" And I think that that action influenced people to the point that they were reflecting on the real face of Somoza. Because the day that Pedro Joaquin Chamorro was buried in Managua, there were 1000's of people walking on the street, going to the cemetery. And the Sandinista Front took advantage of that situation, because they were distributing leaflets and information about the Sandinista Front the same day of the funeral. So it was something that I think had an impact.

And the other situation was that just a few months before the triumph of the revolution, there was a journalist in Nicaragua, and I don't know if he was from ABC or NBC, but he was working for one of these big TV companies, news companies in the US. And he was covering the insurrection in one of the neighborhoods in Managua. And when he was doing his work, one of the National Guards was so upset the journalists were showing what was happening in Nicaragua that he just killed him. And when they killed this journalist, whose name was Bill Stewart, Bill Stewart is the name of the journalist, he was killed, and also his translator, because he was with a translator in order to have the interviews.

But what the National Guard didn't know was that just a few blocks from the place this was happening, there was a camera person who was recording. So that same night, when people knew that there was a journalist that was killed in Managua, Somoza was giving a press conference saying, "We want to take this opportunity to let the world know that a terrorist group of the Sandinista Front, they have killed a journalist in one of the neighborhoods in Managua." And what he didn't know, what was in the in the same press conference, there was another journalist, and he said, "You're lying. You're a liar, because here we have this video where you can see how the National Guard is killing our colleague."

And that short film was broadcast the same day in the US, and it had an impact on the US public opinion. Because then President James Carter, at that time, the next step he did was to cut the military aid from the US to Somoza. And just a few months later, Somoza fell down. He was defeated by the Sandinista Front. So I think it was a combination of many different factors, but I can remember those specifically. And after that, the Sandinista Front defeated the US.-backed dictatorship in Nicaragua.

Ken Yale: So you've really given us a great historical context. So now I'm thinking back to where we started. Here you are, you're watching all of this celebration in the street. So now we understand why everybody is out celebrating. The many, many years of US intervention, the human rights violations of the Somoza dictatorship, these heroic actions. And then here you are, and now you are interested as a youth in becoming more involved in the Sandinista movement. So what was the process of joining the FSLN as a young person at that time?

Harold Urbina: First, when I was in primary school, when I was 10, I joined the Sandinista Children Association, and I was there in my two last years of primary school, in ‘83 and ‘84. Then in 1985 when I became a high school student, I was assigned a task to gain the right to become a member of the Sandinista Youth. And I was for more than one year, the chief of one Brigade of Nicaraguan Explorers, which was like the Nicaraguan version of the Scouts in the US and some other parts of the world, and I was doing that work. And after finishing that task, I was granted with the affiliation to the Sandinista Youth. I was not an active member yet, just an affiliated person.

Then the next year, the second task I was assigned was to be part of the Secondary Student Federation, and I was the vice president of my classroom for one year. And at the end of that year, I went to pick up coffee in the southern part of Nicaragua. And over there, I was a member of what we called at that time Production Student Brigade, which was a format we were using to involve high school students with people in the countryside, to make a connection between urban areas and rural areas. So we understood how coffee was produced by being right there in the farm picking coffee in the fields.

So when I returned from that experience at the beginning of 1987, which was my third year in high school, they said, "Now we have another task for you. You were already the chief of the Brigade Explorers. You were already the vice president of this classroom. Now we are going to move to another level. We want you to be a candidate and to be the president of the afternoon session." Because in my high school, the name of my high school was Ramirez Goyena. So in the Ramirez Goyena High School, we did have a morning session and an afternoon session. So in the afternoon session, we were about 500 students. So my task was to be in charge of the organization of the students in a population of 500 people. So you can imagine, for a person who was at that time just 14 years old, the level of responsibility that it was to be in charge of all of the student life and activities in that specific school. And I did it.

So by ‘87 I won my recognition to become a militant of the Sandinista Youth. And then the next year I was asking again to be part of the Secondary Student Federation, and that was like my responsibility. And in fact, I was working as an active member of the secondary Student Federation between 1987 until 1989. And in 1989 I was the president of my whole high school. I'm talking about a population of almost 2000 students, and I was already 16 years old. So by that time, I was really, really involved in in the Sandinista Youth, plus my work in the Secondary Students Federation. And I was getting ready for going to war, because in the 80’s, we did have a special law of the military service. So all of the kids between 17 years old to 25 years old, they needed to have two years of mandatory military service, and I was happy to enroll in the Nicaraguan army, the Sandinista Army at that time in order to go to the mountains and defend our revolution.

But in 1990 we lost the elections and we were really disappointed and frustrated. I still remember that in December 1989 the US army invaded Panama. Ken Yale: I'm wondering about what you learned when you lost the election, and in particular, this concept of reconciliation and unity, and then how that showed up afterwards in the practice of the FSLN?

Harold Urbina: Yeah, it is important to mention that when we lost the elections in 1990 and we started to work again from the grassroot level, we understood how important it was to recognize that your neighbor is your brother, your sister. And since we were coming from a period of war when the Nicaraguan society was polarized a lot, and we were able to see the division among family members, we thought, "No, if you want to return to power, we need to establish partnerships and alliances with former enemies." And we were researching, we were reviewing the lessons from Sandino. And Sandino was the one who was saying, "No, no in Nicaragua, we don't care about conservatives or liberals. We care about the whole Nicaraguan population." He was saying, "We are all equal. It doesn't matter if they are from different political parties. It doesn't matter if they are from the rural area or from the city." So he was giving us this lesson since the past, and we were thinking, "Oh, Sandino is right."

And the right-wing people realized that they were not getting anything from the neoliberal government. They were saying, "Yes, we have been always attacking the Sandinista Front, but at the end of the day, they are right. They are just trying to promote rights of the people. So I think that we should give the Sandinistas a second opportunity to return to power." And for instance, we were having a very important health project called Miracle Mission in the Sandinista municipalities back in 2000, or even before 2000. We were in an alliance with the Cubans and with the Venezuelan government. We were sending people from Nicaragua, especially from remote areas, to have surgery because they were having some eye problems, like cataracts, and I forget the name of the other disease. And the Sandinistas were sending first the former enemies. They were saying, "I will come to prove that I don't have any reason to take revenge. We have this program and as I can see that you are sick, we will send you in our group." And they were also offering the opportunity to learn how to read and write to the former contras and former people against the Sandinista Front.

And all of this movement happening at grassroots level was changing the minds of people towards the idea they had of the Sandinista Front. Because another point is that back in the 80's, many of the Sandinistas were criticized because they were following the ideas of Marxism. And they were saying, "You are all against church. You are all against the people that believe in God, because you're Marxist." But then the Sandinistas were saying, "No, no, we are not against Christianity. We are not against church." And they started a dialog with all of the sectors of society. It was an open dialog for many, many years, until the point that when we were organizing our strategy for the elections in 2006 we came with a change. And when the Sandinista Front was going to register in the electoral council to be part of the election, we came and said, "No, no, this time, instead of writing ‘Sandinista Front for the National Liberation’ now you are going to register this." And the name was “Alliance: Nicaragua United Triumphs," "Allianza: Unida Nicaragua Triunfa." That was the title of the Sandinista Front within the ballot of the 2006 election, which meant that you didn't find the name Sandinista Front in the name of the alliance. The name was an alliance to succeed based on national unity.

And that's why, when the Sandinista government returned to power, then we said, "This is the Government of National Unity and Reconciliation," like following the previous idea of the alliance that Nicaragua United was going to succeed. It was a really complex process, because even myself, I was against the idea of shaking hands with contras. And it wasn't until I saw Daniel Ortega talking to former enemies, even saying, "Please forgive me," when you when you see a leader doing something like that, you understand the real meaning of reconciliation. And then we started to replicate the same action at the local level. It was complicated, but I think that for that reason, many contras and many right-wing leaders, they became supporters of the Sandinista Front. And it was something really complicated. Perhaps I'm forgetting some examples right now, but I can come back to this question later.

Ken Yale: It seems that part of what made that reconciliation possible was the accomplishments of the FSLN. Because obviously, if you weren't meeting the needs of the people, this reconciliation wouldn't have happened. So what are some of the things that you're most proud of that the FSLN has accomplished that helps even people who may not be supporters of the FSLN see that you're acting in the interests of the people?

Harold Urbina: I think that since the time when the Sandinista Front returned to power, they have been working on what we call the process of Restitution of Rights. They came and said, "since this moment, we are going to have education and health services free of charge." With that single action, people were able to see that we are really changing. And in addition to that, many of the social sectors that were excluded in the past started to have a voice again and to participate. And the Sandinista Front was not excluding, as I told you, anyone. Even former enemies were included in the whole social policies that the government was implementing.

But I also want to mention something else that is not perhaps related to people receiving a material benefit for being part of a such a project or program, and that was the commitment of the Sandinista Front with Peace. The Sandinista Front came back to power saying we will do as much as possible to keep this nation in Peace. Because the Sandinistas said, "We were not able to develop Nicaragua enough back in the 80's because we were in war. But we are sure that now that there is no more war in Nicaragua, people will see how our country can develop." And obviously economic growing started because we're not in war. Then, since we were working in reconciliation, we didn't have any violence problem in the streets or any polarization. So we were growing. We were developing. And even the Catholic Church and the private companies, they were not supporting the government, but not attacking, which was a big change.

And in a few years, we were able to recover many of the rights we lost in the neoliberal years. And people were really happy. Because it doesn't matter if you are not a Sandinista member, but if you see how your children can go to school free of charge, in primary, high school, and even go to university without paying any money, there's no way that you will not acknowledge that improvement. And you see that the roads that go to remote areas are in good condition, and that small farmers can take their production out of the community because of the Sandinista government, it's building new roads everywhere. So people say, "Oh, I'm satisfied with this, and I'm not a Sandinista, but I need to acknowledge what they are doing." And the Sandinistas never said, "Oh, we will do this to convince our opponents to become Sandinistas." They have never said that. It has been the opposite. It has been, "No, we feel okay to live with the rest of our community. It doesn't matter what they think, what they believe in."

And I think the local people, have been able to acknowledge not just the development in the communities, but the environment of peace and safety. Citizen security in Nicaragua is a big thing, because Nicaragua is in fact, the safest country in Central America. And that's because the National Police and The National Army and every institution in Nicaragua is doing its work based on this Family-Community Model. So when it comes to citizen security, your own neighbors are protecting you. It is not that they are informers for the police. It's that they just can come to the police and say, "we care about our neighborhood, and we have noticed that we have some strangers living in our neighborhood. They don't talk like Nicaraguan people. What about if you just take a look at them, just to make sure that they are not any threat." Things like that. And that's why we don't have this penetration of the gangs that come from Guatemala, El Salvador, or Honduras, because the community itself, plus the National Police, are really making sure that Nicaragua is a safe country.

And just to give you an example, since Nicaragua is in the middle of Central America, and we have the Pan-American highway coming from the north, going to south and vice versa, I have heard the testimony of big trucks drivers. When they cross the border in the north from Honduras and they enter Nicaragua, they say, "Oh, see there is a patrol of the Nicaraguan Police is right there." So they say, "Oh, we feel safe," because they say that when they are driving in Guatemala, Honduras and El Salvador, they feel threat, not by the gangsters, but they feel threat by the police, and they see our police and our army as an entity that will give protection to anyone inside our territory. Because it doesn't matter if you're not Nicaraguan, once you are here the Nicaraguan police and army, they will protect anyone. That's something different. Because I myself, when I go to Honduras and El Salvador, I feel a little scared of the police and the army over there.

Ken Yale: And what can you say about the lessons learned and accomplishments of the FSLN in terms of the role of women, and how that has helped to build support for the revolution?

Harold Urbina: Yeah, I think that the Nicaraguan revolution has been led by youngsters and by women. And since the very beginning, Sandino's wife was an active member of the Army for the Defense of National Sovereignty. So since that moment, we have always been able to see the important role of women in the struggle. In our revolution, the role of women is not for decoration. It's not an ornamental role. They do have an active role and they have gained that space, not just because we have these guerrilla members before the triumph of the revolution, but because when the Sandinista revolution was in place, many, many women became active, participating in everything at the different levels, at the same level as men. That's not something new.

What the Sandinista Front has done recently, since 2007 until 2025, is that we have legalized all of these rights that women have. Because they won these rights by their own struggle, but now the government is offering them the law that correspond to the rights they have. And that's how we have many women in top positions in the different ministries. We also have women in top positions in the police and in the army. At the community level, we have community leaders, the majority of them are women. And when it comes to the presidency of Nicaragua, Daniel is the Co-President, but Rosario Murillo is also the Co-President. So we have a system of 50 and 50 for everything. And there are some spaces where women are more than 50%, they are 60 or 65% in the participation process.

So I think that's something that makes Nicaragua different from the rest of the countries in Central America, because women are not passive. Women are not submissive. They fight, they fight for their rights. And education is important because we have been able to talk about the rights of women at a family level, at a community level. We have it in the school programs. We have this topic in the university study programs, so it's something comprehensive.

And I'm not saying that we don't have problems, because we do have problems. There is a permanent struggle against violence, violence against women and children. But that is something that is happening everywhere in the world. But here we are trying to do the more we can. And perhaps the difference can be also seen in the way we call it. Because in the US, I know they call it "domestic violence." When you say "domestic," it means something that is happening inside your house. It's like a private problem. But here in Nicaragua, it's a social problem. It's something that you can even find in the work of different institutions. Because I think that for the last 25 years, we identified violence against women as a social problem, as a health problem, a difference from other places where it's a private thing. Here, everyone has a commitment to do something when they know that there is a case of domestic violence.
