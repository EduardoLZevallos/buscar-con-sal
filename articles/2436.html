Grit in my teeth - How I flipped my board in a wild ride down a volcano in Nicaragua

Submitted bytortilla onVie, 06/04/2018 - 14:08

Cassandra Jaramillo, Dallas News, April 5th 2018
https://www.dallasnews.com/life/travel/2018/04/05/thrills-spills-volcan…

The image of me with my lime green sled atop an active volcano in Central America is a picture that I can’t wait to show my grandkids one day.

Rushing down pebbles on a 1,600-foot-high volcano called Cerro Negro will be my wildest memory of my visit to Nicaragua. 

Before I went down the volcano, I repeated the safety tips several times: Keep a strong grip, lean forward to slow down, use your heels to brake. 

None of it mattered.

Once I shimmied my board to take off on the descent, I felt gravity pulling me faster and faster. The adrenaline rush was so intense that I did the opposite of what I was told and leaned back, which propelled me to speeds as high as 40 mph.

That’s when fear started to settle in. I’m so scared of heights that I get anxious when I reach the top of a staircase, but somehow I managed to get the courage to sled down a volcano.

I panicked and somersaulted midway through my descent. My oversize overalls failed to protect my forearms, which got scraped up. My heart pounded so hard I could hear it in my ears. I didn’t feel any other pain besides small stings from my cuts.

“You OK?!” shouted spectators at the bottom.

I raised a thumbs-up, got back on and finished to the sound of applause from those who had been worried.

This was my first time in this Central American country, and every local I met told me I had to venture to Cerro Negro, since it is one of a few places in the world for volcano boarding. It's become a must-see attraction that's part of the country's tourism resurgence,  along with eco-tourism at wildlife preserves and beaches. 

I have never been one for extreme sports, but I consider myself a relatively active person who enjoys distance running and hiking.

Completing Cerro Negro didn’t sound as extreme as bungee-jumping off a building or skydiving. The idea was basically pitched as sledding, with tourists clad in safety gear riding down on a board. It sounded like the perfect introduction to extreme sports.

But I quickly found I could have better prepared myself for a grueling day that ended with a 40 mph wipeout.  It's not for young kids or the faint of heart.

Booking Cerro Negro

While in Nicaragua, I stayed in the capital of Managua with a plan to do day trips to several cities in one weekend. I was short on time to visit Cerro Negro, which is about 2 1/2 hours away, so I booked a private tour guide to meet my boyfriend, Dom DiFurio, and me at a time that worked with our schedule.

Whether you book a private or group tour, expect to pay from $35 to $50 for the visit. You can pay extra for help carrying your equipment.

Booking our tour guide cost $40 each, but he did not offer transportation. It’s difficult to get to Cerro Negro if you rent a car and aren’t familiar with the area. The terrain is very hilly, with loose rocks that are hard to handle in a regular car. My guide agreed to meet us in the city of Leon, and we drove out following his directions. Our SUV, a Hyundai Creta, barely made it there.

Before the hike

To put this hike into perspective for North Texans, going up Cerro Negro is like climbing Reunion Tower -- twice. And Leon is known to be hot, with temperatures usually ranging from the mid- to high 90s. You need to be well-hydrated beforehand, and you need to bring a liter of water, along with sunscreen. Luckily, it was cloudy on the day we trekked up, but I still felt extremely thirsty throughout the hike, despite the milder sun.

I didn't expect the hike to be as steep as it was. Just know that it is entirely normal to get out of breath, so take a break as needed. It took us about an hour to get to the peak of Cerro Negro, which our guide said was impressive, but it can take some up to three hours.

Safety gear

The ride began with pure happiness, a feeling that I had finally conquered my fear of heights as the wind blew through my hair and hit my face. But that turned into fear that sunk into my stomach when my speed reached a point of no return. As much as I tried to slow down using the instructions the guide gave me, I couldn’t.

I’ve never gone so fast on something as small as a 3-foot piece of wood.

That’s why it’s important to ask what safety gear is included when booking your tour. Do they provide overalls? Do the clothes come in various sizes? What about knee pads, helmets, goggles, mouth scarves? Yes, volcanic rocks will end up in your teeth. Had I thought ahead, perhaps I wouldn't have a few small scars as souvenirs.

As tempted as daredevils might be to ride the board standing up, don't do it. Snowboarding on ice is so different from boarding Cerro Negro's volcanic rock. Just sled down.

I promise the adrenaline rush won’t disappoint.

If you go

Online: Travel blog ViaNica is a good source of information on volcano boarding and more for your trip.

Price:  $35 to $50.

Dallas Morning News reporter Cassandra Jaramillo was in Nicaragua for two weeks in February for a fellowship with the International Center for Journalists.
