Message from President Comandante Daniel to the families of Nicaragua

January 11th 2021

Nicaraguan brothers and sisters, Nicaraguan Youth, today now we are beginning, already we are in the first days, first weeks of the month of January 2021, here in this historic Park, before the Monument to Ruben Dario, just a few weeks away from the Commemorations of the Birth of Ruben in the month of January, on the 18th, and then, of Ruben's Passing to Immortality in the first days of February, we salute all Nicaragua's young people, Nicaragua's Youth, with that magical phrase of Ruben: "Youth, Divine Treasure!"... Youth, Divine Treasure!

Rereading Rubén's Poems, Rubén's Political Writings, his analyses, his reflections, they are still valid now; that is to say, they are valid for our People, for Nicaragua and they are valid for Our America. Because he well expressed in his political writings, in the Chronicles he wrote from Buenos Aires, from Spain, from different places, in which he is constantly pointing to, in his own words, he speaks of Imperialism; he uses that word, Rubén, "Imperialism".

He speaks, with that vision of his, with that wisdom of his, about the United States then... imagine, Ruben writing, analyzing, in the last quarter of a century of the 1800s and the beginning of 1900s, analyzing what is happening in Nicaragua, and what is happening in Latin America, and acknowledging the power of the United States, and explaining how the United States, with so much power, could make a great contribution to the Peoples of Our America.

But Rubén already points to the Monroe Doctrine, and he points out how the United States, which, with the Monroe Doctrine, committed itself to defend Latin American Countries from any extra-regional aggression, instead the Monroe Doctrine became an instrument for the United States to attack our countries, and that when an extra-regional power, England, not by chance the very same England that invaded the Malvinas, occupied the Malvinas and provoked the war in the Malvinas, the very same England, at the very time when that Treaty was already in place, by which, in these new times, the United States was committed to supporting any Latin American country against the aggression of any extra-regional power.

And just as the United States became England's accomplice in the invasion of the Malvinas, against Argentina's People, became informants, allies, facilitators of the aggression against the Argentina's People in the Malvinas, Rubén at that time could point logically to what he was experiencing; he criticized the US, he questioned them, he condemned the United States, because when the English landed here in 1895, they arrived here, in Corinto, the United States simply did nothing, what they did was to seek agreements, agreements with the English, to divide up the region's territories.

Rubén indeed has the virtue, it is true, of being an extraordinary poet, who with his poetry revolutionized the Spanish language, revolutionized literature, poetry, but he also in his poems set out positions criticizing US American Policies, US American aggression, and he knew how to point this out in different circumstances. He targeted Roosevelt with the poem he dedicated to him.

Before that, he had written a poem in which he referred to the power of the United States, the strength of the United States, that no one can doubt, but urging that the United States put all that strength to accompany, to support the countries of Latin America. That was the message that he left behind and one that he had hope for at that moment, even with the criticisms that he made; but then too, Dario pointed out once again in depth the practice of North American policy, unmasking once more in depth what the United States interventionist policy in Our America really was.

Here is a quote from a moment when President Roosevelt was in France, in Paris, giving talks, talking about law, talking about humanity, talking about values; at that moment, what Dario wrote led a brother writer, Federico Henríquez Carvajal, of the Dominican Republic, to write:

"Ruben Dario, the famous poet, who was Nicaragua's Minister in Madrid, was in Paris when Mr. Theodore Roosevelt was a guest of France, as well as most other European nations." All of Europe was entertaining Roosevelt. "And while the indefatigable ex-President received, in official or scientific circles, all sorts of demonstrations of support and sympathy, and while the universality of the newspapers greeted the skillful statesman with not a few hyperbolic phrases in honor of the leader of American Imperialism"

In other words, Europe praising the Empire, the Empire was nothing more than the extension and expansion of Europe in America. Because it was Europeans who invaded the North of Our America, and it was the Europeans, Anglo-Saxons in the first place, who murdered the owners of those lands, our ancestors too, in the United States. And it was the Europeans who decided, for themselves and among themselves, to take over these lands. It didn't cost them a peso, they didn't pay a cent. What they did was kill the Indians who refused to be enslaved, and who fought, and were systematically killed.

The Dominican Brother says, in these conditions "let the voice of the illustrious Nicaraguan be heard, serene and insinuating [...] to say to this very powerful head of the Republican Party of the American Union", this President at the time was a Republican, "that he be just and use his influence to respect of the sovereignty of the State of Nicaragua. It is a civic defense of his country, and with it of all Latin American peoples, while at the same time such a forthright appeal to the great federal nation, in the person of Mr. Roosevelt, in favor of international morality and the august rights of free peoples, of constituted, sovereign and equal States, though for now small and weak. This page, this gesture, honors Dario".

Words of this Dominican writer praising the attitude of Dario, speaking for Nicaragua. So you see Rubén Darío in those times speaking for Nicaragua against Yankee intervention. In these times now, all Nicaraguans should be united, speaking against the Yankee Intervention in Our America, and in Nicaragua; against the military maneuvers that are being carried out now there in territorial waters that legally, by law, those waters have to be delimited according to international law, in accordance with previous resolutions and treaties between Venezuela and Guyana.

And what is the U.S. government doing right now? Deploying its war fleet with the complicity of the Guyanese authorities, in what is a flagrant violation of international law, because those waters cannot be transited by any country, because they are waters currently in the process of defining how and what corresponds to the sister Bolivarian Republic of Venezuela and what corresponds to Guyana. That is what is in the treaties, in the agreements, and they are not respecting the treaties or the agreements.

Or what the United States is doing now, which is naming the sister Republic of Cuba, incorporating the People of Cuba, the Cuban Families, in their list of terrorist States, that promote terrorism, which encourage terrorism. In other words, we see a US Government with a US President who continues to deal blows against international law and order, when he is obliged to respect international law and order, not just law and order in the United States, but also internationally. But this is a President who is well known for having trampled on international law and order.

There in the United States they have their contradictions and we have seen how US American society has become polarized in the United States with the last elections. And we could say, it seems, because of what is happening in the United States, that the US electoral system needs profound reforms. Because what is happening is that, first of all, they still have a system that was established when there were states with a small white population and a large black slave population.

Then came the idea of proportionality and that at election time, it did not matter which party had more votes from all the US American People, instead the votes were decided state by state, by their delegates; that is to say, there is no respect for the principle of citizens' the right to vote.

Let's suppose that here we approve an electoral reform where we say that elections will no longer be decided by the vote of the majority of citizens, but that it will be the vote of the delegates of each of teh country's departments, according to the population that each department of the country has. There is nothing democratic about that, and I am sure that the first ones to jump up and down here against a decision like that would be those mentioned by Dario and whom Sandino called "sell outs", "wimps", "quislings".

These would say: "No, you can't do that!" So then we would tell them, but if we are copying the system of the United States which you say is the perfect system, because you have said that it is the perfect democracy. You have sold the idea of the "perfect democracy", when in reality it is a bipartisan dictatorship decided by economic power, by the power of the big businesses that are in charge of manufacturing artillery, aerial armaments, everything to do with the arms race.

That is to say, this is the power, not to mention the other economic powers that exist there in the United States of America, which are the same ones that at the time, back in those days, Dario also refers to, that brought William Walker here to Nicaragua.

Walker came here, why? Because there were American businesses interested in the transiting the San Juan River, crossing the Lake, to connect from the East Coast of the United States to the West Coast. Because we all know, we have talked about this on other occasions, that there was the breakneck rush of thousands of North American citizens anxious to have either a lot or a little or at least some money and they knew that, in the West, gold had been discovered, and everyone was rushing there, but to travel across the North American territory was dangerous, long and dangerous, because there was still indigenous resistance and there were also many robbers, and the wagon trains were exposed there.

So then, US American businessmen, owners of the Vanderbilt shipping lines, said that here was a natural passage which made it easier, safer and faster to travel from New York to San Francisco, down from New York to the Nicaraguan coast, to Nicaraguan ports, and from there move along that route and then head up to San Francisco and vice versa.

Thousands of Americans passed through here, and because of the disputes between the companies and the disputes between the political parties of the time, which we see reflected here now still, those political parties have not disappeared, they are sellout Parties, they sent for William Walker! And then, of course, what the US Government did was recognize Walker when he was appointed President here in Nicaragua.

And Darío addressed them, although generally, since we were small, since we were children, we are more familiar  with Dario's Poems: "Margarita is beautiful, the sea, /and the wind, / brings the subtle essence of orange blossom... Margarita, I'm going to tell you a story:/ There was a king who had..." And so goes Dario's Poem.

Or "The princess is sad... what will the princess have? / The sighs escape from her strawberry lips,/ she has lost her laughter, she has lost her color./ The princess is pale on her golden chair..."

Or The Wolf's Motives: "The wolf of Gubbia, the terrible wolf", who met with hatred among people, envy, rage, such that the wolf ended up getting beaten and his only recourse was to return to the mountains and continue his life. Why? Because he found that the people of the village did not live together as brothers, but treated each other as enemies; and if they treated each other as enemies, imagine how they treated the poor wolf. The wolf got beaten every day, until the wolf got so upset he returned to the mountain and again started killing sheep, killing cattle.

That is the poem, or other poems like: "It is something formidable the old race saw:/ a robust tree trunk on a champion's shoulder of a champion,/ wild and brave, whose strong mace,/ wielded the arm of Hercules, or the arm of Samson. For helmet his hair, his chest for armour,/ could such a warrior, from Arauco in the Region,/ lancer of the forests, a Nimrod, hunting everything...", and here Darío is referring to Caupolicán, the Great Indigenous Warrior.

Or, "How happy and fresh the morning is!" We used to learn that when we were starting the first grades. "How happy and fresh is the early morning / when the air takes me by the nose; / and a fat and lovely girl,/ grinds corn with a stone".

How many poems we learn at school as children, and then when we grow up some of us explore Dario's writings a little more; but making a foray into Dario's political writings is really something impressive.

Here is this book of Dario's Political Writings; there are the other books of the poems, which are extraordinary, unquestionably. But in these political writings one can really see the genius of Darío who did not limit himself to producing the wealth of his literary revolution, but also dedicated himself to the struggles of our People, the struggles of Nicaragua.

You see, this matter became controversial, although it had the support of the majority of the population, but it became controversial a few years ago, now in the time of our government. if you look closely, it deals with the Canal across Nicaragua, and refers to the treaty that the United States imposed on Nicaragua, whereby the country lost all rights, and it refers to the deputies of Nicaragua's Congress who happily approved that treaty.

They are the same ones that in 1990, in the National Assembly, approved but without managing to comply with all the legal formalities, the deputies of the UNO government, at the request of the UNO government, a government imposed by the United States of North America and the first thing it did was to say that Nicaragua its right, awarded under the judgment of the International Court of Justice, to be indemnified with billions of dollars.

In the same way, Darío criticizes the deputies, who are the same, their behavior is the same as those who now want to get control the National Assembly, they are the very same, just there to do everything the Yankee orders them to do.

Darío says here, look... "American Imperialism is not of today," and he is saying this all those years ago, imagine, more than 100 years ago Rubén Darío was saying: "American Imperialism is not of today, American Imperialism about which so much is spoken, is a phenomenon that has occurred and occurs in all nations that are growing larger, and unable to fit, as it were, in their own territory, they need to extend by the natural law of expansion.

American Imperialism is not of today, as some people believe, it has channels and deep roots in the constitution of the history of that great country, when Spain's American colonies became emancipated, the United States gave their help, of course, recognized them as independent nations, and proclaimed, to defend them from Europe, the famous Monroe Doctrine".

In other words, the Yankees appeared then as if they were going to defend the colonies that they were emancipating themselves; but what does it say later: "The American Government said to Europe that it would be annoyed if the European Powers intervened in the affairs of America, either to make the new Republics return to Spanish domination, or to obtain territorial concessions from them".

Note how they are already deciding about all America, it was already a contradiction with the United States becoming an Empire just like the old European Empire; already the European Empire was weakening and the North American Empire was getting stronger.

"The nascent Spanish-American Republics that were just beginning to be formed were very weak and small; the Motherland had left them like Lucretia's child, naked on the Earth, and also bloodied by wars of Independence.

Monroe's Doctrine established a protectorate over them, and the American hegemony corresponded to the United States, which in those days had a population of about 10 million". In other words, the whole region had populations nothing like the populations of our countries now.

"The most brilliant triumph of American Imperialism," take note... "However, when in 1859 the attempt was made to open up an interoceanic canal across Nicaragua, the United States had to recognize England under the Clayton-Bulwer Treaty". That is, there the Empires agrees, above all the Empires of the United States, of England.

"England recognized the United States the right to build the Canal wherever the Washington government wants, and to close it in time of war, according to the treaty". You see, they imposed a treaty, this is the most brilliant triumph of American Imperialism in those times. Look at it, they imposed it on all of America and on the British by its supporting the treaty.

"The English and the Yankees, without being close friends, seem to be understanding each other lately for the domination of the World," as they understand each other now in these times. The Yankees go and attack a country with so-called sanctions, the English follow, and then the Europeans follow as well. Yes, take a good look.

Then again Rubén says here, calling the Knox Note, "an attack against the sovereignty of a People". This is a book that really contains a wealth of what Rubén lived, what he saw, and what he pointed out, and what he discovered, and what he denounced. Because he ached for our Peoples, for Our America, he wanted Central American union and he said so in as many words, here are Dario's writings and poems about the Central American union, and also on Latin American and Caribbean unity.

And we, consistent with our historical roots, and therefore with the Living Thought of our indigenous Ancestors, with the Living Thought of those who defended Nicaragua from the Yankee invaders in San Jacinto, of Dario's Living Thought, of Zeledón's Living Thought, of Sandino's Living Thought, of Carlos. That is to say, if we set out on a route through our history, we are going to find thought that is really alive and is so living and so up to date, that it tells us with absolute clarity to continue fighting, and not to giving up, but to continue fighting. This is what Dario teaches us, all of us.

And seeking, as Dario did, seeking that the United States, as well as Europe, understand that we do not accept being anyone's colony. That they let us get on with our work. That they let us develop our programs, our programs that have to do with the transformation of health, education, recreation, public investment in human development, sports, expanding coverage and quality of  water and electricity services, in which we are already approaching 100% coverage of electrical energy; roads, highways, bridges, ports, airports.

Who would have thought that there would ever be an airport in San Juan de Nicaragua? That was a little place lost there, and practically facing the sister Republic of Costa Rica, that is, they are separated by the river. So, if they needed to study, they crossed to Costa Rica, if they needed to buy things, they crossed to Costa Rica, if they needed fuel, they crossed to Costa Rica, if they needed medical attention, they crossed to Costa Rica.

With our Revolution, with the Darian Revolution, because this is a Darian Revolution, the Sandinista Revolution is a Darian Revolution, we were able to incorporate these communities and this symbolic town that was known as San Juan del Norte, we were able to integrate it into Nicaragua, and now there are schools there, there are health posts, there are gas stations, there is food supply so that people can buy right there.

They are even now coming over to buy from the Costa Rican side as well, because they find that it is cheaper to buy in Nicaragua than to buy in Costa Rica. And that's not bad, it's good, the exchanges that take place in the border areas we support them, we promote them, both in the communities that border Honduras, as well as in the communities that border Costa Rica, and also the Caribbean Communities that border the Caribbean Islands.

We continue to strengthen the legalization of properties. We have given thousands of property titles to rural families and other families who lived in their houses, who had small plots of land and had built a house there, but did not have a title deed.

Imagine the insecurity that a family goes through, especially the head of the family who does not have a title deed, and who is threatened with eviction by scoundrels, who use lawyers to find a way to evict them. And in that regard we have managed to help thousands and thousands of Nicaraguan families, without asking them what political party they are from, or who they voted for. They are simply Nicaraguans, they are Nicaraguan sisters and brothers and they have the right to a plot of land, a piece of land for their home, or a plot of land to cultivate, in the case of rural famililes.

Likewise, all the works we have developed on the Caribbean Coast, an area that was abandoned and where we have titled all the communities of the original peoples that live there on the coast: the Miskito communities, the Mayagna communities, the Ramaquíes, the Afro-descendants.

There, we have distributed titles in a territorial extension of more than 35,000 square kilometers. To give an idea, the Salvadoran brothers and sisters with a population a little larger than ours live in a territory of 20,000, 22,000 square kilometers. So the Caribbean Coast is a large territory. But in addition, there on the Coast we have modernized the airports; in Bilwi it goes without saying, and also in Bluefields, and in Corn Island.

And there on the Coast, the miracle of the highway has com about, which after centuries finally unites the Pacific population with the Caribbean population and the Caribbean population with the Pacific population. This road was built with much love and dedication by the workers, a road that was difficult to build because it rains a lot there and you have to take advantage of the times when the rains abate to be able to get to work.

There the Minister, our Minister, General Oscar Mojica, you'll have seen him, he doesn't stay in his office, he goes around supervising the works, the roads, the bridges. At all times, he has not stopped for a single day, even in the most difficult moments of the year 2018, there he was inaugurating highways, roads, and that is where this extraordinary work was built, not of asphalt because asphalt does not resist the rains well, it is easily destroyed, but of concrete. So it's already a solid road.

And on the road to Bilwi, for that whole region of Puerto Cabezas, which has its capital in Bilwi, we are working there to build the bridge. The bridge over the Wawa River is already planned, over what is an immense river that for now must be crossed with a barge, and when it rains a lot the river overflows and even the barge gets displaced, is diverted, and traffic stops.

But in addition, we have already advanced in what were known as the "quagmires", which was a transit point in the Minign Triangle where transport regularly got stuck, especially in the rainy season. And what did we do? That's where we first experimented with concrete. Then, where the roads were quagmire, we didn't try asphalting them because the rain would wash it away, but not concrete, so, concrete; so we managed to overcome that transit point.

And now, new routes that we are also developing to move from the other parts of the Mining Triangle to Bonanza, and from Bonanza to Waspam, to Bilwi; In other words, to cover all of the mining towns, Rosita, Siuna, Bonanza, and then, to move ahead with a route that is going to promote great development in the area, towards the very tip there, heading for Cabo Gracias a Dios, where the great storm occurred with the sail ships in which Columbus came, and that in the midst of that great storm they found relief there because the waters were calm, there was no storm, and that's where the name comes from, Cabo Gracias a Dios.

And there we have Waspam as the most populous point and with the most economic development, and the communities a along the river; that is, joining the Pacific also by that route to the River Coco, to Waspam. With the Highway it is much faster, what we export can go by the highway to Bluefields, and what we import can travel from Bluefields to Managua or to any other city where the cargo may be going.
